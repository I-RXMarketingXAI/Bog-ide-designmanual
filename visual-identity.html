03 Visual Identity

The visual system.

This chapter defines the core visual language used across campaign work, retail communication, and digital surfaces. The intent is not just consistency, but recognizability with calm confidence.

Key Rules
  • Use the logo with calm breathing room.
  • Let red feel intentional, not omnipresent.
  • Use serif for statement and sans for utility.
  • Prioritize hierarchy before decoration.
  • Choose imagery that feels human, not generic.

Logo

The mark should always feel deliberate and well placed. It is a signature, not wallpaper.

Preferred

Bog & idé

Placed with room to breathe on light surfaces.

Usage Principles

  • Keep the logo clear of competing text and decoration.
  • Use approved color versions only.
  • Scale with restraint in editorial layouts.
  • Reserve the facade-specific version for building or signage contexts.

Color

The palette should feel warm, cultivated, and legible. Red carries attention; neutrals carry the reading experience.

Typography

Display Role

Serif for statement.

Use an expressive serif voice for page titles, chapter openers, and focal messages that need emotional weight.

Utility Role

Sans for clarity.

Use the sans layer for body copy, labels, navigation, specs, and anything that needs fast readability.

Graphic Devices

Signature Framing

Use framing gestures and corner treatments selectively to create recognition without overwhelming content.

Rule of Restraint

If typography, imagery, and message are already strong, graphic devices should step back rather than compete.

Imagery

Works Because

Human and atmospheric.

Images should convey reading, gifting, curiosity, presence, and tactile warmth.

Avoid

  • Cold stock imagery that could belong to any retailer.
  • Overly staged product scenes without mood or context.
  • Cluttered image systems with no focal hierarchy.
